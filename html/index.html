Introduction

When you maintain an enterprise project with lots of subsystems and services you probably have several teams. Each team can work on one or more services or applications, have different capacity and seniority level. People from one team can be moved to another for many reasons: personal interests, communication, special knowledge or capacity needs and so on. Newcomers should be integrated into the development process as soon as possible to reduce time and cost, so you should find the shortest way of introduction not only into project terminology and functional requirements but into details of technical implementation as well. You may have skilled developers but internal and cross teams communication may essentially impact on development speed and quality. Your project will probably have a legacy which should have been upgraded a couple of years ago and external integrators from time to time will complain about unclear interfaces and request support thereby increasing the workload of the team. All these questions will bring you to the idea of technical and solution management in different teams. Highly likely, it depends on the size of your project, you will have a couple or even a team of architects to resolve challenges above. One of the goals of architectural runway is to provide standards for technology landscape and common patterns. In this article I tried to express my thoughts on five basic aspects which should be kept up-to-date for successful project maintenance and development.

Application

If you have decided to build a technological foundation in your project the first topic in queue to review is your applications. Here are included all services which communicate with each other; end-user and administration interfaces (public and internal); custom tools for supporting data, infrastructure, development and testing processes. When you gather information about all implemented elements you will find technologies, approaches and techniques, patterns introduced by different team members.

Based on gathered information about technologies and practice evolution in general, you will be able to define core concepts for your future template application(s). This should be done on different levels: frameworks and libraries usage; application structure and internal configuration; implementation of generic functionality, e.g. logging, communication interfaces, basic validation, caching, etc; common concepts, for instance exception concept; design patterns. One of the important things here is to stop on time: not every good idea you have should be added to the core template. Sometimes it’s better to document it and provide an example of a good approach (e.g. you see a good approach of implementation of business rules checks: particular rules most likely shouldn’t be in template application so even good validation service implementation won’t make sense). We will come back to the documentation topic later.

Probably your template application at some stage will provide lots of important functionality. Here you should split it into a showcase application, modules (to simplify update process for teams) and template itself. This will help to keep the template clean from usage examples which should be deleted each time you clone it. While splitting modules don’t forget that it will bring you additional maintenance complexity. It depends on used technologies, organization and team structure and processes. Topic of structure and processes is out of scope of this article, but you also should think about it when introducing new technologies (e.g. the process of subscription and license approval will affect the speed of modern service or fancy library introduction into development process).

Testing

To keep high quality of your delivery you need a testing strategy. It’s rather hard to cover all aspects of a large system with manual review. With the growth of the project you will have to increase the QA team, find out and hold a ratio of developers to QA engineers. Tested area and its complexity can be changing on different phases of the project’s life cycle. There is a risk of overloading the QA team. To secure quality you have nothing to do but introduce automation. You can define your own strategy or apply any common one like testing pyramid. The main idea is to keep a clean and transparent understanding of process and structure. Automation will make your QA team’s life easier and comparison of regression and fiesta will sound less sarcastic.

From a technical point of view you need to think about transparent metrics, tests classification, test framework, reporting and analytics tools which will be integrated in your ticketing system. You should provide your QA team development and configuration management support to prepare testing areas in your ecosystem. Only collaboration of these three can bring your team to clean processes and architecture. Each one of them will be a customer and implementer for two others at the same time. Based on the designed solution it will be possible to clarify and unify testing approaches in particular teams.

Overall testing process can be centralized or decentralized… or both at the same time on different levels, e.g. each subsystem can have its own testing processes but on level above you probably will need integration, E2E, load tests and data checks for the whole chain. There are four major points to think about: strong classification, unified processes, independent runs and results gathering. Presence of the first one is a basic condition. When you have your tests classified you will be able to exclude duplicates, optimize concept implementation and reduce overall testing time.

Infrastructure

We use infrastructure every day and it can make the development process easier or vice versa turn it into hell. Everyone can take the best tools, services and hardware but those who can integrate them all together to provide a safe and reliable ecosystem for stable solution development are much less. Infrastructure provides a major impact on processes you have. Infrastructure is about communication between technical components, people and data.

To provide developers benefits but not problems you should think about how they can maintain their code and documentation, manage environments, use external services and deliver value to end-users. Process of getting access to particular secure spaces should be clear and fast: who would like to wait weeks for access to a specific area when the development team becomes distributed? But at the same time it doesn’t mean that you should grant access to everyone from everywhere… You should support such practices like code review, static analysis, continuous integration and delivery — almost no modern project can live without it.

From an exploitation point of view critical are data backup, security of public interfaces and monitoring. I guess you’ve already heard a joke about two types of administrators: those who don’t make backups yet and those who already do. When you maintain a bunch of components don’t forget to look after vulnerabilities reports and exploits distribution. Transforming one of your public services to a botnet spam node, for instance, is the most harmless thing that can happen. Set up metrics and alarms to prevent system outages.

Configuration

Each element of your infrastructure needs to be carefully tuned and what is worth to think about is how to organize configuration update and rollback processes. Versioning of your configuration files and scripts probably will help you but it’s not enough — there should be an isolated space for configuration implementation and testing. Allocate some percentage of resources to increase reliability. This will work at least for two reasons: experiments with configurations won’t affect your working ecosystem and you will be able to apply changes only after getting successful results. Definitely, there are possible extreme cases when you don’t have an option because it’s almost impossible to duplicate everything.

There are three major components on application level: configuration of application itself, environments configuration and processes configuration. First two are tightly linked and the third one provides a path to application distribution within your infrastructure. Developers know what to tune in their application and more or less can understand how to tune different components, but the exact values cannot be set up without configuration management team support. Probably you would like to include automation (and not only of course) testing to the distribution process here you definitely won’t be able to dispense with the QA team. Proper configuration will specify how to establish fast and clear delivery and help to comply with the nonfunctional requirements.

Different teams have different requirements for configuration according to their process and developed software specific. So most likely exactly the same automation of the distribution process cannot be applied, but common actions and helper functions can be combined to meet the need. Also you can provide some top level steps with hooks implementation and manual actions possibilities. Number of stages also can differ and here it should be clear how they should be mapped to other solution components.

Documentation

↑

The last but not least topic is documentation in a broad sense. All we are doing as developers is solving business problems and description of your work makes it transparent and reasonable for your customers. Documentation helps to clarify what tons of hours were paid for and why it was necessary. What matters is the benefits which business will get: costs reduction, faster delivery, higher quality — the classical triangle. Direct connection between your solution and the following positive impact should be shown in reports.

The technical audience of documentation should be able to find answers to the core question: Should we use the solution? It is quite a logical question when there are teams with already established development approaches. Don’t hesitate to provide clear instructions on approach usage and migrating existing code base and processes to new rails mixed with guidelines and common problems solutions description. Teams should understand the positive effects on their daily work they will get if they adopt the approach. Solution design specification will help you to find its weak points, involve engineers into improvement processes — get feedback and maybe support with development.

It is worth to clarify rules and templates for documentation forms used on a daily basis such as: code documentation (self documented code usually exists only in imagination), specification of APIs and user stories, howtos. Sometimes teams should be pushed to adopt the ongoing documenting process to share experience, save knowledge and inform on usage of what they produce. It may be painful or not depending on the tools provided, fortunately, there is an abundance of them.

Summary

Article does not and can not cover all possible aspects and scenarios, and you will definitely have your special case. Topics mentioned in the article are highly coupled. Changes in one area affect others which makes concepts implementation unique in each particular situation. Nevertheless common practices (like InnerSource for instance) and patterns can be found, adapted depending on demands, applied and integrated into daily activities to achieve results of successful project evolution. Of course smart architecture is not enough: amount of legacy code and its quality, team involvement and motivation, business readiness for improvements should be taken into account as well. Implementation looks like a complicated endless process and it is, but a journey of a thousand miles begins with the first step. Even small adjustments can make a team’s life a little bit easier.
